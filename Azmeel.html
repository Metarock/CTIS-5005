Azmeel's Ethical Reflection

I believe that artificial intelligence (AI) and robotics is ever-evolving in our society, with it looking to play a massive role in our daily lives in the future. It will play a big part in making things easier for humans, as the technological sector will only continue to improve and refine the many software’s and hardware it creates, especially in the health sector’s surgical field. AI robotics in surgical health can provide many opportunities and choices for surgeries to improve through autonomous surgical machines. Currently, any form of robotics relies on the manual capabilities of a surgeon. However, this can all be changed by introducing AI robotics, allowing for better and safer surgical techniques without any human error. An example of this could be in aid of suturing, as the number of mistakes that AI can cause is less than that of a human and during the suturing process. The amount of tissue that is removed by the machine is significantly smaller than the traditional surgical method; meaning that the recovery time is shorter and that the scaring left behind is reduced substantially (Shademan et al., 2016). Through this, I can see that AI can make things easier not on.ly for the surgeon who can reduce their operation times by half simply by relying on this procedure but also the patients who are being operated on by the machine can be assured that it won’t get tired or lack precision during the surgery. Therefore, increasing the chances of the operation being successful. The choices that AI in robotics can provide to the populous is by allowing surgical machines that are currently operated using humans could be converted to a complete autonomous surgical machine by implementing AI. An example is the Davinci machine, which has four arms, each fitted with a surgical instrument to help carry our surgical procedures (Ngu et al, 2017). Implementing an AI into the system of machines would allow surgeons to step back and all the machines to complete the surgery while they observe the procedure. The choices AI can give us are boundless, primarily how it can be implemented in the existing machines that we have now. Another option AI provides to the patient is choosing whether they wish to be operated on by either a human or a machine, allowing them to have freedom of choice and comfort when they are wheeled into the operating room. I believe it is imperative that the patient get to choose how they wish to get their operation done so that the patient can feel comfortable when going through the procedure instead of feeling anxious.

However, just as there are positive aspects present in AI robotics so are negatives, with the risks, especially regarding ethical breaches that can occur from either the hospital providing the procedure or the manufacturers who create it. Doing my share of research for the topic allowed me to see just how many implications can occur, especially when it comes to the software in technology which is not always full proof when implemented. I can explain this by looking at the ACM Code 2018 Task Force (2018), which explains how a professional should push to create high-quality work and avoid software malfunctions or incorrect predictions. This especially holds true during surgery; because such a scenario could cause catastrophic results, such as during mid-operation. An example of this is the self-driving car created by Google, which crashed into the side of a bus due to an incorrect prediction made by the AI program (Harris, 2016). This point only emphasises that AI programs will never be full proof, meaning that surgical centres and hospitals will need to consider things like this if they wish to adopt the autonomous procedure as it will come with its risks. Another ethical issue is whether the AI program is robust enough to stand against external threats, such as malware and cyber-attacks that may target hospitals. An example of this has occurred in New Zealand’s Waikato Hospital, which had all its systems shut down due to a ransomware attack (Leahy, 2021). If such an attack were to occur while the robot was in surgery or was being set up for one, could pose a severe safety risk due to the harm it can bring to the patient. This may only prove that manufacturers of the robotic system need to make the AI's security system a primary concern due to the increased cyber-attacks on medical facilities by making sure to continuously test and patch vulnerabilities in their systems. Hence, their software is always up to date.

Lastly, I see that the greed of monetary gain poses the most considerable ethical risk. Meaning that manufacturers who try to push their product into the market without rigorously testing their system may cause harm by not explaining any limitations or potential problems that may rise from their software. This was emphasised through the US healthcare algorithm which favoured white patients needing extra medical care over black patients, with black patients having varied healthcare costs compared to white patients who suffered from the same conditions. This racism continued into the justice system, where an algorithm called COMPAS had access to the US court database, which predicted the likelihood of those who would be a repeating offender. The system predicted 43% for black offenders and 23% for white ones (Shin, 2020). If such an AI system was to be implemented further into the healthcare system without proper testing and trailing could pose significant moral and ethical risks to the patient that are under the machine. This would only build upon the bias that already exists in our society by favouring specific groups for better care compared to others, therefore breaking the moral code of a healthcare worker which build on the trust, integrity, respect and honesty they must show to the patient.

Overall, I believe that the implementation of AI will be a massive game-changer in the healthcare field. However, as the ethical challenges are ever-present would mean that manufacturers should make sure their systems are robust enough and give fair treatment to all patients. Furthermore, suppose healthcare centres choose to adopt their AI. In that case, they should make sure it will follow the same professional and moral codes a human would, as the surgeon will always have an obligation to care for their patient, so should the machine adopted to take their place.
